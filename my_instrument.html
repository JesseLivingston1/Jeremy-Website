The Story of My Bass Violin

Long before the violoncello, there was the violin. It emerged as an instrument suited for dance music with its explosive articulations and vibrant sound. As time went on, the violin gave birth to the viola which in turn gave birth to the bigger viola. The new family of instruments began to be played in bands or consorts which became a popular platform for music making in many parts of 16th and 17th century Europe. Innovators explored the possibility of a still lower instrument of the violin family and created what was called the “violone” or bass violin. Able to play a full octave or more below the viola, the possibilities of violin band music and beyond were greatly increased. The instrument was played in many stringings (between 4 and 6 strings), tunings, and bowings. It was played on the floor, between the legs, on a stool, or tied with a rope around the player’s neck. At the 18th century rise in popularity of the new small violone, in Italian literally “violoncello,” many of the old basses were cut down in size to keep with the fashion. The bass violin I play is modeled after one such instrument. The King, made by the master Italian craftsman Andrea Amati, was created in the middle of the 16th century. It was part of a collection of instruments for the court of King Charles the IX of France. Ornate paintings of the words “Justice” and “Piety” on the sides and a female depicting justice on the back graced this instrument. But it was later cut down the middle in order to “modernize” to a violoncello, marring the original proportions of the painting on the back. In 1992, when Daniel Larson decided to recreate this instrument in its original proportions, clues could be found in the missing painting as well as other documents describing the proportions of bass violins of the time. The larger proportions restore this instrument to its bass origins and give it the dark yet articulate quality that grounded the fiery ensembles of its past.

And for the technical nuts out there, the instrument is strung in equal tension gut made by Dan Larson at a gauge best suited for A=440 in a tuning of G-C-F-Bb from top to bottom. The bow is a short Italian model by modern maker Pieter Affourtit.

A word about my violoncello

The violoncello I play was constructed back in 1998 by Dan Foster, a family friend who also happenedto be a master violinmaker. Many of the years of good musicking I have enjoyed are indebted to Dan’s skilled hands and generous spirit.
